World Electricity Data

Visualisation by Wilbert Chandra

Generation

Overview

Electricity generation is the amount of electricity a generator produces during a specific period of time (1 year here). It is generally measured in GWh or TWh. The map above shows the generation of electricity in TWh by the countries worldwide. A deeper dive to the sources of electricity these countries use is shown by the line chart beneath the map.
A specific country can be selected by clicking the map, which will aggregate the line chart to show the data for the particular country.

Insights

China and the USA continues to be the leaders in electrical generation throughout the years, with India trailing in a distant 3rd. However, information on Russia's electrical generation is unavailable thus actual rankings might be different.

We can see that coal, gas, and hydro are the leading sources of electricity. Recently there has been a steady rise of solar and wind, as we aim towards a more sustainable and green energy option. However, past fascinations with nuclear-based electricity have been abandoned due to cost-effectiveness.

Capacity

Overview

Electricity generation capacity is the maximum electric output an electricity generator can produce under specific conditions. Capacity is generally measured in gigawatts(GW). To put into perspective, one gigawatt is enough energy to power about 750,000 homes.

The pie chart on the left shows a breakdown of capacity shared by continents worldwide. Asia, Europe, and North America hold the most electricity , with Asia almost responsible for half of the worldwide capacity by 2020.

Capacity Distribution

Capacity represents the maximum nameplate output for different fuel types in a country. The area chart to the right represents the distribution of fuel capacity based on fuel types. The trend here mirrors the generation of electricity from these sources. Coal, gas, and hydro takes up most of the capacity to accomodate for their major contribution to worldwide electrical generation.

Emission

Overview

All electricity generation technologies emit greenhouse gases at some point in their life-cycle. As the biggest contributor to CO2 emissions, the burning of fossil fuel have been a main topic in the conservation of the planet.

The steam graph to the right shows the share of emission created from different sources of electricity around the world. Metric tons of carbon dioxide equivalent or MTCO2e is the unit of measurement in this graph. The unit "CO2e" represents an amount of a GHG whose atmospheric impact has been standardized to that of one unit mass of carbon dioxide (CO2), based on the global warming potential (GWP) of the gas.

Relationship to generation

The bar to the right shows the evergrowing electrical generation and CO2 emissions, which both mirror the same upward trend. Although the world is shifting to renewable power, it is still aparent that our efforts have not mitigated the increase in CO2 emission from energy generation.
